Research

How the brain sees.

Vision feels effortless. It is also one of the most computationally demanding things a brain does. I study the neural code that turns sensory input into robust, generalizing perception, and the intelligent behavior it makes possible.

The neural code of intelligent behavior

Biological vision is intelligent because of what it allows an animal to do. It generalizes zero-shot to novel scenes, recognizes objects under unfamiliar lighting, and remains robust to nuisance variables that still derail current AI systems. I want to understand how the brain implements these computations.

I study how single neurons and populations of neurons in the visual system convert high-dimensional sensory input into a code that supports behavior. My work spans the retina to the cortex, in mice and primates. The results that compel me most are those in which apparently complex activity resolves into simple, interpretable structure.

Why now

The tools to address this question have only recently become available. Large-scale recordings now capture the activity of thousands of neurons simultaneously across the visual hierarchy. AI models trained on these data, including foundation models of neural activity and digital twins of individual visual areas, predict a neuron’s response to arbitrary inputs with high fidelity. These models remain largely opaque internally, in the same sense that AlphaFold is opaque, but they are cheap to query at scale.

A predictive model returns a neural response for any input I provide. That makes it possible to run millions of in silico experiments on the visual system. I use them to synthesize most-exciting stimuli for individual neurons, sweep continuous stimulus parameters, and dissociate variables that no real experiment could disentangle. Each result generates a candidate hypothesis. I bring the most promising ones back to the animal for empirical validation. The hypotheses that hold up become the foundation for mechanistic, biologically grounded models that can be manipulated to make causal predictions, including in disease.

Record, predict, hypothesize at scale, verify, build mechanism. I expect this loop to define the next decade of vision neuroscience.
